Understanding Your Cardiovascular Risk Assessment

You've received two different risk assessments for your cardiovascular health. This tool will help you understand the difference between these assessments and guide you in making an informed decision about your care. Hover over the two icons below to learn more about each score.

Traditional Score

Framingham Risk Score

Framingham Risk Score (FRS)

What it measures: Your 10-year risk of having a major cardiovascular event (like a heart attack or stroke).

How it's calculated: Uses traditional factors including your age, sex, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, systolic blood pressure, smoking status, hypertension medication use, and family history of early cardiovascular disease.

AI Score

AI-Based Prediction

AI-Predicted Risk Score

What it measures: Your risk of cardiovascular events using artificial intelligence analysis of multiple factors.

How it's calculated: Uses advanced AI models that can analyze a wider range of data, including traditional factors plus additional information like medical images, genetic markers, detailed lab results, and social determinants of health.

What to expect: This decision aid will take about 15-20 minutes to complete. You'll learn about your risk scores, compare your options, and identify what matters most to you. You should complete this aid before your follow-up consultation and be prepared to discuss your decision with your clinician.

What does this difference mean?

Traditional Score is Higher

It flags a risk based on established and proven factors contributing to cardiovascular disease such as age, gender, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking status, etc., which is a key priority for established clinical guidelines.

Traditional Score is Lower

It suggests your basic, well-tested risk factors are low, but it may be missing subtle risks it wasn't designed to see. This may include your genetic markers and medical images.

AI Score is Higher

It suggests a strong signal was found in both the proven factors (like age, cholesterol, etc.) and your complex data (like a subtle abnormalities in a medical image) that increase your risk.

AI Score is Lower

It suggests the data processed proves that your overall health profile is low risk, despite basic risk factors analyzed by the traditional score.

Understanding the Trade-offs

üìä

Traditional Score (FRS)

‚úÖ Advantages

  • Well-validated through decades of research.
  • Easier to understand and explain.
  • Standard approach in medical practice.

‚ùå Disadvantages

  • Uses a limited number of variables (e.g., age, cholesterol).
  • Assumes a particular pattern of association (linear), potentially limiting generalizability.
  • Less precise for individual predictions; may miss high-risk patients.

‚úì What We Know

  • Typically achieves accuracy (AUC) of 0.74 to 0.76.
  • Proven effective in large populations as the established standard.

? What We Don't Know

  • May not capture all relevant risk factors like imaging biomarkers or genetics.
ü§ñ

AI Score

‚úÖ Advantages

  • Shows statistically superior predictive accuracy in research studies.
  • Better at identifying missed cases (reducing false negatives).
  • Considers many more factors (median of 21 used for predicting).

‚ùå Disadvantages

  • Hard to explain the reasoning behind the prediction.
  • Only about 20% of models are externally tested, and almost none in clinical trials.
  • Potential for bias and poor performance in different populations.

‚úì What We Know

  • Can achieve accuracy (AUC) up to 0.91.
  • Can identify more complex patterns that conventional models might overlook.

? What We Don't Know

  • Long-term outcomes and real-world effectiveness are not yet fully proven by prospective trials.

Key Point: Neither score is perfect. The traditional score is well-tested but uses limited data. The AI score is more accurate in research by seeing complex data, but it is not yet proven in real-world clinical practice. Your decision will depend on how you weigh these trade-offs.

Your Treatment Options

Below are your three main options. Each option includes advantages, disadvantages, what we know about it, and what we don't know about it.

üìÖ

Option 1: Continue Monitoring (Status Quo)

This option means not making major changes now. You would continue with routine checkups and risk assessments unless symptoms or new results appear.

‚úÖ Advantages
  • You avoid medication side effects such as muscle pain or stomach discomfort that some people experience with statins.
  • This option costs less in the short term.
  • It gives you time to observe whether your risk changes with future appointments.
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Cardiovascular disease often progresses silently for many years. Studies show that many patients develop significant plaque before symptoms appear.
  • If the AI model is correct and detects risk that traditional scores miss, waiting may mean missing the best window for early prevention.
  • Subtle abnormalities detected by AI could represent early disease that traditional tools cannot see, especially since traditional scores use fewer variables.
‚úì What We Know
  • Traditional scores such as the Framingham Risk Score are widely validated and used for millions of patients. They guide medication decisions supported by strong clinical evidence.
  • Regular monitoring is a standard pathway in preventive cardiology for patients who are low risk.
? What We Don't Know
  • Many AI models have not been tested in real-world clinical trials. Only about one in five models has undergone external validation, and none have been tested in prospective trials that follow patients over time.
  • AI models may detect patterns linked to higher risk, but we do not yet know whether acting on these predictions improves outcomes.
  • Because most AI research is based on U.S. and East Asian datasets, accuracy for people outside these groups is still unclear.
üçé

Option 2: Lifestyle Changes Only

This option focuses on improving your cardiovascular health through diet, physical activity, stress management, sleep, and smoking cessation, without starting medication or undergoing additional tests.

‚úÖ Advantages
  • There are no medication side effects.
  • Improving diet and activity is beneficial. It reduces blood pressure, improves cholesterol, lowers inflammation, and helps with weight management.
  • Empowers you to take control.
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • It requires sustained commitment and motivation.
  • Lifestyle changes may not lower risk enough if your risk level is truly high.
  • If the AI model is identifying hidden abnormalities such as early arrhythmias or early ventricular dysfunction, lifestyle changes alone may not address these issues.
‚úì What We Know
  • Lifestyle changes are strongly supported by decades of clinical evidence.
  • Many patients with mild to moderate risk improve their cardiovascular health significantly through diet and exercise programs.
  • Even when medications are recommended, lifestyle improvements remain essential.
? What We Don't Know
  • Your individual response to lifestyle changes may vary. Some people see large improvements, while others see only small changes in risk levels.
  • Without repeat testing or imaging, it is difficult to know how your risk changes over time.
üíä

Option 3: Aggressive Intervention (Medication/Proactive Testing)

This option involves starting preventive medication early, undergoing additional cardiovascular tests, or both. It aims to reduce your risk as much as possible.

‚úÖ Advantages
  • This is the most proactive approach. It can reduce the chance of a heart event by lowering cholesterol, stabilizing plaque, and lowering blood pressure.
  • AI-enhanced tests can identify conditions long before symptoms appear.
  • Some AI models have shown extremely high accuracy in research settings, with accuracy (AUC) scores as high as 0.991 for identifying major cardiovascular diseases.
  • Early treatment may be especially helpful if the AI score is capturing risk that traditional tools miss.
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Medications like statins can cause side effects, including muscle aches, increased blood sugar, or liver enzyme changes.
  • Advanced tests may be time-consuming, uncomfortable, or costly depending on insurance.
  • There is a possibility of over-treatment if the AI score overestimates risk.
  • Additional tests can create stress or anxiety. Wearable monitors, imaging, or repeated follow-ups may feel burdensome.
‚úì What We Know
  • Statins are proven to reduce the risk of heart attacks and strokes, especially in people with elevated cholesterol or high traditional risk scores.
  • AI-enhanced imaging tools often outperform traditional scoring systems in accuracy.
  • Some advanced testing methods can match or surpass the accuracy of physicians with over a decade of experience.
? What We Don't Know
  • It is not known whether acting on AI predictions improves long-term outcomes, because no AI model has been tested in prospective randomized trials.
  • It is not clear whether early medication based on AI signals alone provides additional benefit beyond traditional guidelines.
  • It is still unclear how often AI-identified abnormalities progress to clinically important disease.
